How to Price Your Amazon Product for Profit (Not Just for Sales)

New Amazon sellers often price their product by looking at competitors and picking a number that feels "competitive." The problem is that competitive pricing and profitable pricing are two different things — and a lot of sellers don't discover the gap between them until they've already ordered inventory. This guide walks through a pricing approach that starts with your actual costs, not the competition's listing price.

1. Start With Your Break-Even Price, Not the Competitor's Price

Before looking at what anyone else charges, calculate the lowest price at which you don't lose money. This means adding up:

  • Product cost (manufacturing + shipping to Amazon's warehouse)
  • Amazon referral fee (varies by category, often 10–15%)
  • FBA fulfillment fee (based on size and weight)
  • Estimated storage cost
  • Any fuel surcharge or seasonal fee adjustments

Whatever number comes out of that calculation is your floor. Any price below it means you're paying Amazon to sell your product, not the other way around. This sounds obvious, but it's the single most skipped step for new sellers who get excited about a product idea and skip straight to "what should I charge."

2. Build a Target Margin on Top of Break-Even

Break-even isn't a pricing strategy, it's a survival floor. On top of it, you need a margin that actually makes the business worth running. A common target range for FBA products is 15–30% net margin after all fees, though this varies heavily by category and competition level.

If your break-even price is $14 and you're aiming for a 25% margin, your target selling price needs to land closer to $18–19, not $14.50. The gap between break-even and target price is what funds PPC advertising, returns, and the inevitable slow months.

3. Then — and Only Then — Look at Competitors

Once you know your floor and your target, competitor pricing becomes useful context instead of your starting point. There are three scenarios:

SituationWhat it means
Competitors price above your targetGood sign — room to price competitively and still hit your margin
Competitors price near your targetWorkable, but differentiation (listing quality, reviews, brand) matters more
Competitors price below your break-evenRed flag — either they have better supplier costs than you, or they're not actually profitable either

That third scenario catches a lot of new sellers off guard. Seeing a competitor selling at $12 when your break-even is $14 doesn't always mean they're beating you on efficiency — sometimes it means they're losing money too, possibly to buy market share or clear inventory, and that price isn't sustainable for either of you.

4. Account for the Fees Most Sellers Forget

Beyond referral and fulfillment fees, a realistic price needs room for:

  • Returns: even a 5–10% return rate eats into average realized revenue per unit
  • PPC advertising: especially in the first months, ACOS (ad cost as % of sales) can be significant
  • Seasonal storage spikes: Q4 storage fees roughly triple compared to the rest of the year
  • Removal/disposal fees: if a SKU doesn't move, eventually it costs money to remove or destroy it

A price that looks profitable on paper using only referral + fulfillment fee math often turns out tighter once these are factored in.

5. Re-Check Your Price Periodically, Not Just at Launch

Amazon fee structures change — the 2026 fuel surcharge is a good example of a cost that didn't exist a year earlier. A price that was profitable at launch can quietly become break-even or worse if you never revisit the math after a fee change or a shift in your ad spend.

A simple habit: recalculate margin whenever Amazon announces a fee update, and at minimum once per quarter even if nothing's changed, just to confirm your numbers still hold.

Final Thought

Competitive pricing without knowing your break-even is just guessing with extra steps. The sellers who stay profitable long-term are usually the ones who price from their own cost structure first, and treat competitor pricing as one input among several — not the whole strategy.
