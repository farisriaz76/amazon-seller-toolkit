Amazon FBA Fees 2026 Explained — How Much Does It Really Cost to Sell?

If you're selling on Amazon FBA, the price tag on your product is not what you actually earn. Between referral fees, fulfillment fees, storage charges, and the newer fuel surcharge, a big chunk of every sale goes straight back to Amazon. This guide breaks down exactly where your money goes in 2026, so you can price products correctly and know your real profit margin before you ever place an order.

1. Referral Fee — Amazon's Cut of Every Sale

Every item sold on Amazon pays a referral fee, calculated as a percentage of the selling price. The rate depends on the category:

CategoryReferral Fee
Most categories15%
Furniture10%
Beauty & Personal Care10%
Clothing & Accessories12%
Amazon Devices / PCs8%
Amazon Device Accessories45%

This is usually the single biggest fee you'll pay, so getting your category right matters — misclassifying a product can quietly eat your margin.

2. FBA Fulfillment Fee — Pick, Pack, and Ship

This fee covers Amazon picking your product, packing it, and shipping it to the customer. It's based on size tier and weight, not on price. A small standard item might cost around $3.22 to fulfill, while large or bulky items can run well over $10.

The takeaway: lighter and more compact packaging directly lowers this fee. If your product is borderline between size tiers, it's worth checking whether trimming the packaging shifts it into a cheaper tier.

3. The Fuel Surcharge — A New Line Item in 2026

As of April 2026, Amazon added a fuel surcharge equal to 3.5% of the fulfillment fee. It's small individually, but on high volume it adds up — and it's one more reason to recalculate your margins regularly instead of relying on numbers from last year.

4. Storage Fees — Pay to Keep Inventory in Amazon's Warehouse

Storage fees are charged per cubic foot, and they roughly triple during the holiday quarter:

  • January–September: around $0.78 per cubic foot for standard size, $0.56 for oversize
  • October–December: around $2.40 per cubic foot for standard size, $1.40 for oversize

This is why overstocking before Q4 can quietly damage profitability — inventory that sits an extra month in November costs far more than the same inventory in July.

5. Putting It All Together — A Real Example

Say you sell a small home product for $24.99, costing you $6.50 to manufacture, in the "most categories" tier (15% referral), small standard size, weighing 1 lb:

  • Referral Fee (15%): -$3.75
  • FBA Fulfillment Fee: -$3.40
  • Fuel Surcharge (3.5%): -$0.12
  • Estimated Storage (1 month): -$0.04
  • Product Cost: -$6.50

Net Profit: roughly $10.68 — a margin of about 43%. That looks healthy, but change just one variable — say the product jumps to "large standard" size — and the margin drops noticeably. This is exactly why checking the real numbers before listing matters more than guessing.

6. Quick Tips to Protect Your Margin

  1. Always double-check your product's exact category — referral fees vary a lot
  2. Optimize packaging to stay in a lower size tier whenever possible
  3. Avoid heavy Q4 overstocking unless the sales velocity justifies the storage cost
  4. Recalculate your margins whenever Amazon announces fee changes — don't rely on last year's numbers
  5. Factor in returns and FBA removal fees for a fully realistic picture, not just the listing-day math

Final Thought

Amazon FBA can be a genuinely profitable channel, but only if you know your real numbers. A product that looks profitable at first glance can turn into a break-even product once every fee is accounted for. Run your numbers before you order inventory, not after.
